BRIEF HISTORY

Tarlac is the most cultural-diverse province of region three, gaining its name as the "Melting Pot of Central Luzon." Its neighboring places: Pampanga, Pangasinan, Bulacan, Zambales, Nueva Ecija, and acceptably Bataan, made the province a meeting point for these natives, which consequently made it a colorful, plentiful mixture of cultures, especially cuisine. The proximity of one another is also a significant contributor to the shared-history, pre and post-colonial eras. The province is composed of 17 municipalities, namely Anao, Bamban, Camiling, Capas, Concepcion, Gerona, La Paz, Mayantoc, Moncada, Paniqui, Pura, Ramos, San Clemente, San Jose, San Manuel, Santa Ignacia, and Victoria, and its only component city is also named "Tarlac."

The story has it that the province's earliest settlers came from Bacolor, Pampanga, reasonably farmers that fertilized the soil and reap crops. With the rapid road construction and hence population, Tarlac became a fast-growing civilization. Also, the indigenous tribes of Aetas are noted to have been a major contributor to this farming boost, and they commemorate these sentiments by holding the "Malatarlak" Festival, honoring the grass, a talahib weed, from which the name of the province was derived. Tarlac's other nickname is "Sugarlandia of Central Luzon," having its eastern plain a suitable soil for sugarcane planting. Official recognition as a province was accomplished by the Spaniards, cutting out Tarlac from the provinces of Pampanga and Pangasinan in 1874. This explains the logic that these two groups and their languages dominate the cultures and dialects of the province.
